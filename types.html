Pediatric Cancer Types

Learn more about the most common types of pediatric cancer

Leukemia

Incidence Rate
28% of all childhood cancer
Tumor Location
Occurs in blood and bone marrow
Age Group
Common in children and teenagers of all ages
Symptoms
  • Bone and joint pain
  • Fatigue
  • Weakness
  • Pale skin
  • bleeding/bruising
  • Fever
  • Weight loss
More Information

The most common types are acute lymphocytic leukemia (ALL), which is the most common childhood cancer, and acute myeloid leukemia (AML) The symptoms of these leukemias include:

Acute leukemias, such as ALL and AML, need to be treated quickly, often with chemotherapy.

Brain / CNS

Incidence Rate
26% of all childhood cancer
Tumor Location
Occurs in brain and spine (central nervous system)
Age Group
Common in children and teenagers of all ages
Symptoms
  • Headaches
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting
  • Blurred/double vision
  • Dizziness
  • Seizures
  • Trouble walking
More Information

Spinal cord tumors are very rare, and most CNS tumors are in the brain, especially the lower parts like the cerebellum and brainstem.

Lymphomas

Incidence Rate
8% of all childhood cancer
Tumor Location
Occurs in lymph nodes and other lymph tissues (tonsils/thymus)
Age Group
Most common in young children older than 3yo
Symptoms
  • Weight loss
  • Fever
  • Sweats
  • Fatigue
  • Swollen lymph nodes
More Information

There are 2 main types, which both affect children and adults.

  1. Hodgkin Lymphoma, which accounts for about 3% of childhood cancers, is more common in adulthood and is rare in children younger than 5 years old. The disease and treatment are very similar in adults and children.
  2. Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma, which accounts for 5% of childhood cancers, is more common in children than Hodgkin lymphoma, but is still rare in especially young children (less than 3 years old). This cancer is more varied between adults and children and grows quicker in children.

Neuroblastoma

Tumor Location
Occurs in nerve cells from fetus and embryo
Age Group
Most common in infants and young children less than 10 years old
Symptoms
  • Swelling of the abdomen
  • Bone pain
  • Fever

Wilms Tumor

Incidence Rate
5% of all childhood cancer
Tumor Location
Occurs in 1 or both kidneys
Age Group
Most common in 3 or 4 year olds
Symptoms
  • Swelling or lump in the abdomen
  • Fever
  • Pain
  • Nausea
  • Poor appetite
More Information

Wilms tumor, also known as nephroblastoma, is uncommon in older children and adults.

Rhabdomyosarcoma

Incidence Rate
3% of all childhood cancer
Tumor Location
Occurs in cells that develop into skeletal muscles
Age Group
Most common in young children less than 10 years old
Symptoms
    • Local pain
    • Swelling (a lump)
More Information

Rhabdomyosarcoma can start anywhere in the body because skeletal muscles are all over our bodies. Some common areas it can start are:

  • Head/neck
  • Groin
  • Abdomen
  • Pelvis
  • Arms
  • Legs

It is the most common type of soft tissue sarcoma—any tumor that starts in soft tissue like muscle, fat, fibrous tissue, or other supporting tissue—in children.

Bone Cancer

Incidence Rate
3% of all childhood cancer
Tumor Location
Occurs in bones
Age Group
Most common in older children and teenagers
Symptoms
  • Bone Pain (which worsens at night)
  • Swelling around the bone
  • Vomiting
  • Blurred/double vision
  • Dizziness
  • Seizures
  • Trouble walking
More Information

There are 2 main types of bone cancer in children:

  • Osteosarcoma is the more common of the 2 types and occurs most in teens. It usually starts in fast-growing bones like the ends of leg and arm bones.
  • Ewing sarcoma is the less common of the type and occurs in young teens. It often starts in bones in the pelvis, chest wall, or middle of the leg.

Retinoblastoma

Incidence Rate
2% of all childhood cancer
Tumor Location
Occurs in the retina in the eyes
Age Group
Most common in young children around 2 years old
Symptoms
  • White color in eye
  • Poor vision
  • Eye redness
  • Eye swelling
  • Eyes appear to be looking in different directions
More Information

Retinoblastomas are often found because of discoloration in the pupil. The pupil (the dark spot in the center of the eye) usually looks red under a light. However, in a patient with retinoblastoma, the eye looks white or pink.
