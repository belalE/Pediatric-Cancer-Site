Pediatric Cancer

Where biomedical and data science research intersect to save lives.

A Devastating Disease

Cancer is the 2nd leading cause of death for children

Globally, more than 300,000 children are diagnosed with cancer each year

60% of childhood cancer survivors experience life-threatening conditions in adulthood

Every 3 minutes, a child is diagnosed with cancer

1 out of 5 children in the US diagnosed with cancer will not survive

Annually, approximately 16,000 children are diagnosed with cancer in the United States

Most Common Types

Leukemia

  • 28% of all childhood cancer
  • Occurs in blood and bone marrow
  • Most common in children of all ages

  • The most common types are acute lymphocytic leukemia (ALL), which is the most common childhood cancer, and acute myeloid leukemia (AML) The symptoms of these leukemias include:

    • Bone and joint pain
    • Fatigue
    • Weakness
    • Pale skin
    • bleeding/bruising
    • Fever
    • Weight loss

    Acute leukemias, such as ALL and AML, need to be treated quickly, often with chemotherapy.

Pediatric vs. Adult Cancer

Rarity

Differences

Solution

Due to the rarity of childhood cancers, studies and clinical trials usually have limited cohort sizes. So, finding meaningful results is more difficult.

Pediatric cancer displays unique biological characteristics that are different from adult cancers. Thus, they require dedicated research and specialized treatment.

To overcome these 2 challenges, research institutions and children's hospitals must collaborate in multi-instituional studies and work together to harmonize their datasets.

Data Science Tools

UTSW - Pediatric Cancer Data Core

The Pediatric Cancer Data Core is a collaborative project between UTSouthwestern Medical Center, Children's Medical Center, other research insitutions, other hospitals, and other oncology groups. The focus of the project is combining and harmonizing pediatric cancer patient data (both clinical and genetic) for use in future research. The data comes from various clinical trials, and has been standardized with a defined and research data model. Along with the patient data, the website features several tools for cohort discovery and data analysis. On the left is an example of a bubble chart showing copy number variation in the genes of pediatric neuroblastoma patients.

PeCan - St. Jude Cloud

The PeCan platform by St. Jude Cloud is a powerful international index of pediatric cancer data, including cell, protein, and genomics data. The data comes from St. Jude's Children's hospital as well as collaborating institutions from across the globe. The platform features interactive tools to visualize pediatric cancer gene mutations with other factors, such as cancer diagnosis or phenotype. For example, they have cluster maps for RNA data, pie charts for mutations, and graphs of proteins with the number of mutations in each gene.

UCSC - RNA Profile Cluster Map

This cluster map from UCSC interactively maps cells from various types of cancer based on their RNA profiles. The different colors correspond with different cancer types, and the clusters show similar RNA profiles. Through the use of UMAP and t-SNE, clustering algorithms, the map visualizes the relationships between the large groups of cell samples.

UCSC Xena - Genomics Data Heatmaps

UCSC Xena is a platform that "allows users to explore functional genomic data sets for correlations between genomic and/or phenotypic variables." The website contains data from many datasets for a variety of cancer types, adult and pediatric. On the left is a heatmap illustrating the relationship between the expression of the ERG gene (and its consequent RNA sequence) and the copy number variation in the genes ERG, TMPRSS2, and chromosome 21 among prostate adenocarcinoma patients.
